Searching for Task-Specific Vision Paths

Evolutionary block pruning across two vision-language model architectures

Qwen2.5-VL and SmolVLM2

Abstract

We study whether vision-transformer blocks can be skipped at inference time without meaningfully damaging model accuracy. At a fixed skip budget, evolutionary search finds stronger routes than simple layer ranking on both tested architectures. The harder result is equally important: capability-specific routes do not transfer reliably across datasets, so they should not be treated as universal layer maps.

1. Main findings

+4.91 pp

SmolVLM2 evolved generic K4 compared with independent ranking.

+8.90 pp

SmolVLM2 evolved task K4 compared with task-independent ranking.

+2.17 pp

Qwen K6 task route compared with its evolved generic route.

-13.6 pp

SmolVLM2 OCR route compared with its generic K4 route on sealed IIIT5K.

Qwen is completed at K4, K6, and K8. SmolVLM2 is a separate K4 replication, and no missing points are interpolated. The evidence supports combinatorial route search, but not a universal capability-to-layer map.

2. Method

Each candidate route replaces exactly K vision-transformer blocks with identity operations. At a fixed K, evolutionary search balances mean loss, worst-source loss, source variability, and collateral capability damage. No model weights are updated.

3. Sealed transfer

IIIT5K was excluded from route selection and opened only after the routes were frozen. The internally selected OCR route performed 13.6 percentage points worse than the generic K4 route, with a paired 95% interval of [-19.2, -8.4]. This narrows the claim: route search is reproducible, but capability labels alone do not define stable pathways.

4. Efficiency

Skipping four blocks creates meaningful savings inside the vision encoder and modest savings for the whole model. SmolVLM2 measured an 8.60% vision speedup and a 4.19% end-to-end speedup. This is an unlocked same-VM RTX 4090 fallback measurement, not an edge-device result.

5. Reproducibility

Every plotted value traces back to committed frozen JSON. The repository regenerates 21 figure files, manuscript tables, and a machine-checkable paper data manifest with one CPU-only command.
